Module 12: Empowering Indigenous Voices and Medias

Learning Objectives

By the end of this Module you will be able to:

  • Analyze the effect of the relationships between Indigenous Peoples, settlers, and the media on the path towards reconciliation.
  • Describe current formalized approaches to reconciliation.
  • Identify the effects of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada.
  • Identify media-focused calls to action from the Truth and Reconciliation Commission.

End of the week check: I understand my role in critically analyzing media about Indigenous Peoples.

Module 12: Empowering Indigenous Voices and Medias

This course has covered heavy, sad, funny, and empowering stories of Indigenous Peoples. Hopefully, it has deepened your understanding of issues of representation of Indigenous Peoples. As it comes to an end, we challenge you to continue to engage in reconciliation. When you consume media, this might include asking yourself critical questions: Whose story is being told? What image is being portrayed? How did the situation get to that point? Why is the government intervening or not intervening? Who is this news benefitting? In other words, who is saying what, to whom, and how, for what purpose and whose benefit? These questions will help you to keep challenging mainstream colonial narratives.

A final thought that perhaps, Wes Studi said best…"Over the years, what's happened is, I think people. and especially Indian people, and other minorities that have been affected by the way that the United States of America was built, we began to put pressure on the storytellers of the world, in that, 'Hey, what about us? Did you uh, did you ever stop to think that no, we didn't disappear. You didn't kill all of us. And we are still here, we have developed, and we have been a large part of your development?' I think we need to start telling stories in a way that is real. Enough of these fairytales. Enough of this making heroes out of people that weren't actual heroes. Let's talk about the fact that our present and our future depends on a better recognition of our past. And I think that some filmmakers, some studios, caught the gist of that and began to move in a direction that is hopefully more true to what our population is.'

Again: "We need to start telling stories in a way that is real. Enough of these fairytales."

Listen to more about this topic here #Oscars.

We end the course as we began, telling our stories. We have travelled full circle since Module 1, from telling our stories, to examining stories about us without us, and back to centring Indigenous voices. We tackled the impact colonial narratives have on Indigenous communities. More importantly, we gained insights into Indigenous worldviews and perceptions in both historical and contemporary contexts. Finally, we built a toolkit to more critically analyze media about Indigenous issues

Why is Indigenous humour important?

Multiple correct answers. For example, humour can be a tool for processing trauma, coping, and community connection.

Why are comedic, joyful, and playful forms of Indigenous media important?

Multiple correct answers. Indigenous Peoples should be represented and portrayed in all and any contexts. Diverse representation allows for the complexity of Indigenous cultures, Peoples and stories to be shared.
