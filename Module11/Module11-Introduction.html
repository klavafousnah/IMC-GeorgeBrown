Module 11: Weaving Through Reconciliation

Indigenous Peoples have always had customary ways of communicating over distances. The most common pre-colonization were petroglyphs (rock paintings) and written language forms (symbols and pictographs – Winter Counts and Leger Art). When messages needed to be sent over long distances, runners would customarily traverse the land to other Nations to deliver them. The Inuit also communicated through rock structures, the now familiar Inuksuit, which were rocks arranged in a particular way to send messages about food caches or caribou routes or to act as landmarks for navigating. Indigenous Nations’ communication practices were sophisticated and ensured that the message/content was shared as accurately as possible. Colonialism not only interfered with these customary communication networks it used mainstream media to circulate colonial narratives that deliberately changed how Indigenous Nations were perceived. And it ensured Indigenous Peoples were given no voice in those mediums.

In this module you will learn how and why Indigenous Peoples are using digital platforms to regain control of their lives and their Nations. You will learn about the ways Indigenous movements unite Nations and voices from all over Turtle Island. And with the help of the video clips, you will begin to identify and examine the hierarchical process that the Canadian government employs when dealing with Indigenous Peoples, and the media’s role within it.
