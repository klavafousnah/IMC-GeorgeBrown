Module 8: Repairing Relationships: An Overview

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module you will be able to:

  • Analyze the impact of media bias and manipulation of information on Indigenous Peoples and their stories.
  • Explain how popular media, especially the news media, uses storytelling to perpetuate the continued oppression of Indigenous Peoples.
  • Identify strategies to improve relationships between Indigenous and non-Indigenous people.
  • Describe current formalized approaches to reconciliation.
  • Identify the effects of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada.
  • Identify media-focused calls to action from the Truth and Reconciliation Commission.

End of the week check: I will be able to evaluate mainstream reporting on Indigenous issues, have engaged in discussion and provided critical feedback on Indigenous issues, and be able to identify issues that Indigenous people and communities encounter in Canadian society.

Introduction to Module 8

Indigenous Peoples' habitation of Turtle Island is at the heart of the conflict between settlers and Indigenous Peoples. When settlers came to Turtle Island, they saw untapped resource potential and desired to develop it all. They failed, or did not care, to see the vast and complex coordination of sovereign Nations that already existed, which included an economic system and shared understanding around territories and movement of peoples. The colonial narrative was that Indigenous people were a “problem.” As Duncan Campbell Scott, superintendent of Indian Affairs, put it:

I want to get rid of the Indian problem. I do not think as a matter of fact, that the country ought to continuously protect a class of people who are able to stand alone…. Our objective is to continue until there is not a single Indian in Canada that has not been absorbed into the body politic and there is no Indian question, and no Indian Department, that is the whole objective of this Bill (National Archives of Canada, 1920). 

The colonial government’s solution to the “Indian problem” was to use government policies to eradicate Indigenous Peoples from existence, in particular, through Indian residential schools. Campbell Scott enforced policies that made attendance mandatory for Indigenous children under the age of 15 years old. This move had devastating impacts on Indigenous communities.

Indigenous Peoples are and have been aware of the mistreatment and negative attitudes directed toward them by settlers. They understand how policies and laws have been put in place to erode Indigenous rights. And they have fought colonization and assimilation in both individual and collective acts of resistance.

This module discusses how Indigenous Peoples have reclaimed their narratives and taken control of their stories, histories, and ongoing relationship with Canada. It also introduces and explores the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) and its investigation to uncover the truth of what happened within residential schools and to reconcile these wrongdoings for Indigenous communities. The TRC generated 94 recommendations (“calls to action”); this module will focus on those related to media.

Why are Indigenous new sources, like APTN, significant?

Indigenous Peoples having control over telling their own narratives takes power back from settlers who have (mis)told Indigenous stories for years. It also allows for the important intergreation of Indigenous cultures and languages into media.
