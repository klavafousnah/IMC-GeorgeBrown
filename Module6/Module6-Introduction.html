Introduction to Module 6

The primary goal of treaties was to deny Indigenous Peoples their right to land and force them into small pockets of territory called reserves. Their lands could then be exploited for resources by settlers. To attempt to justify this colonial project, policies under the Indian Act worked to create narratives that discredited Indigenous Peoples. For example, colonial governments implied Indigenous Peoples were unable to manage their own affairs. They also perpetuated stereotypes about Indigenous Peoples as not intelligent or complex. By doing this, they tried to construct a plausible narrative for settlers that obscured the fact that Indigenous Peoples were the original inhabitants of North America. Narratives fabricated by settlers continue to be used to justify taking resources from Indigenous Peoples’ traditional territories.

In this module, you will watch a documentary on Colten Boushie that illustrates how treaties benefitted government interests. It also investigates the narratives that led to an all-white jury valuing settler ownership of land more than the life of an Indigenous person. This module underscores some reasons why it is critical for Indigenous Peoples to tell our own stories about events in our communities, and explores how to do this effectively.

What is assimilation (in relation to Indigenous Peoples and the Settlers)?

Assimilation is the attempt by settlers and the Canadian government to erase Indigenous cultures and languages by forcing Indigenous peoples to live and speak in western ways of being.

Why is the media so powerful?

Media has the potential to reach and influence mass amounts of people. The media people have access to dictates what information they see and therefore what knowledges they are most familiar with.
