Introduction to Module 4

Nation-to-Nation relations were formally established between the Indigenous Nations and the Canadian government (previously the British Crown) through the establishment of Treaties. Treaties were meant to provide a framework for living together and sharing the land Indigenous Peoples traditionally occupied. They set out the roles and responsibilities for all parties. However, Indigenous Peoples and settlers had different perspectives on how treaties worked and what kind of relationships they created.

For example, the Haudenosaunee’s Two Row Wampum, which shows two beaded purple rows running lengthwise, depicts how two Indigenous Nations were to travel in their own canoes without steering into the path of the other. The Treaties were interpreted in a similar reciprocal way by Indigenous Nations. As discussed earlier, the relationship between land and identity is integral to an Indigenous worldview. However, this interpretation of the Treaties was not shared by the Crown. This module, Nation-to-Nation Relations, and the next module, Nation-to-Nation Narratives, explore the ways that narratives and interpretations of Treaties have shaped understandings of these important formal agreements over time.

The treaties should have established a better relationship with Indigenous People's today, but the diverging interpretation by the Crown and how that has informed the Canadian public's views of what the treaties mean has had dire consequences for Indigenous Peoples and communities. The film, Trick or Treaty, is important to understanding the perspectives about Treaties from settler and Indigenous perspectives.
