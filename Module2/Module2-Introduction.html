Introduction to Module 2

The purpose of this module is to gain an understanding of the significance of Indigenous Knowledges (IK), the construction of settler narratives, and the ongoing colonization of Indigenous Nations. This is an opportunity to begin developing your ability to critically analyze colonial narratives and perceptions that have a detrimental effect on Indigenous communities.

Indigenous Knowledges is a broad term that refers to knowledge generated within a community that is used to guide decision making that will ensure its survival and long-term well-being for the next Seven Generations. To support the ability to make sound decisions, Indigenous Peoples comprehend the world in various ways, including through observation, listening, and sharing stories. That is why the term knowledges is pluralized. This module will provide an overview of Indigenous Knowledges and their important relationship to Indigenous storytelling.

Indigenous Knowledges are multifaceted: they shape Indigenous worldviews and include the body of knowledge around origin stories, ways of life, and ways of being. Indigenous Peoples use a variety of terms to describe unique methods of knowing, being, and doing. Each Nation has its own set of Original Instructions that govern relationships with land and within the community. It is critical to recognize that these can be derived from history, legends, stories, and ceremonial practices. Indigenous Knowledges are communicated over generations through oral traditions, elders, and memory keepers. They record history and communicate ideas such as the significance of carvings and traditional beaded wampum belts used as covenants.

How do Indigenous Knowledges differ from western science?

Indigenous science doesn’t look to create a universal set of answers or explanations but rather focuses more on the exploration of these ideas and is often contextual in nature.

What are Indigenous Knowledges?

"Local and indigenous knowledge refers to the understandings, skills and philosophies developed by societies with long histories of interaction with their natural surroundings. For rural and indigenous peoples, local knowledge informs decision-making about fundamental aspects of day-to-day life." - ICTINC

Why do we refer to them as Indigenous Knowledges?

Because they are place based. There are many of them.
